# Evolution of Artificial Intelligence

Before the IBM PC even existed, AI was already making its first moves.

The very first application is generally credited to Christopher Strachey, who wrote a Checkers (Draughts) program in 1951 for the Ferranti Mark I computer. It could play a complete game at a reasonable speed. It was the first true "thinking" software.

Here are the next seven milestones that paved the way for everything we use today:

1952: The Logic Theorist – Often called the "first AI program," Allen Newell and Herbert Simon created software that could prove mathematical theorems, demonstrating that machines could do more than just "crunch numbers."

1956: The Dartmouth Workshop – This is where the term "Artificial Intelligence" was officially coined. A group of scientists gathered to launch AI as a formal field of research.

1958: The Perceptron – Frank Rosenblatt designed the Perceptron, the very first neural network. It was intended to be a machine that could learn to recognize shapes and images, just like our brains.

1961: UNIMATE – The world’s first industrial robot joined the assembly line at General Motors. It used "taught" sequences to handle hot pieces of die-cast metal, proving AI and robotics had real-world muscle.

1964: ELIZA – Created at MIT, ELIZA was the world's first "chatbot." It pretended to be a therapist and was so convincing that some people actually started sharing their secrets with it!

1966: Shakey the Robot – Built at SRI International, Shakey was the first mobile robot that could reason about its actions. It could navigate a room, move blocks, and "think" its way around obstacles.

1970: MYCIN – One of the first truly successful Expert Systems. It was designed at Stanford to identify bacteria causing severe infections and recommend antibiotics, often performing as well as human doctors.

Here is a chronological breakdown of seven major AI milestones for each decade from the 1990s to the present.

# The 1990s: From Logic to Data (The Rise of Machine Learning)
This decade marked a shift away from rigid "expert systems" toward algorithms that could learn patterns from data.

1991: DART Logistics – The U.S. military deployed the Dynamic Analysis and Replanning Tool (DART) to automate logistics during the Gulf War, reportedly saving more than the total cost of DARPA’s AI research to that point.

1992: TD-Gammon – Gerald Tesauro created TD-Gammon, a backgammon program using neural networks and reinforcement learning that reached near-human championship levels.

1995: Support Vector Machines (SVMs) – The publication of SVM research by Corinna Cortes and Vladimir Vapnik provided a powerful new tool for data classification, becoming a staple of 90s machine learning.

1997: Deep Blue Defeats Kasparov – IBM’s Deep Blue became the first computer to defeat a reigning world chess champion (Garry Kasparov) in a tournament setting.

1997: LSTM Networks – Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber invented Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent neural networks, solving the "vanishing gradient" problem and enabling AI to process long sequences of data.

1997: Dragon NaturallySpeaking – The first commercial speech recognition software was released, allowing users to dictate 100 words per minute to their PCs.

1998: The MNIST Database – Yann LeCun and his team released the MNIST database of handwritten digits, which became the global benchmark for training and testing computer vision systems.

# The 2000s: The Era of Big Data and Robotics
Increased computing power and the explosion of the internet allowed AI to tackle real-world physical and digital environments.

2000: Kismet – MIT researchers debuted Kismet, a social robot designed to recognize and simulate human emotions, a milestone in human-robot interaction.

2002: Roomba – iRobot launched the Roomba, bringing autonomous, AI-driven robotics into the domestic household for the first time.

2004: Mars Exploration Rovers – NASA’s Spirit and Opportunity used autonomous navigation to safely traverse the Martian surface.

2005: DARPA Grand Challenge – Five autonomous vehicles successfully completed a 132-mile desert course, proving that self-driving technology was no longer just theoretical.

2006: Recommendation Engines – Platforms like Netflix and TiVo popularized AI-based personalization, using web activity to predict what users would want to watch or buy.

2009: ImageNet Launch – Fei-Fei Li launched ImageNet, a massive database of labeled images that became the "fuel" for the deep learning revolution of the following decade.

2009: Google’s Self-Driving Project – Google secretly began its autonomous car project (later Waymo), signaling the start of a massive industry race for driverless tech.

# The 2010s: The Deep Learning Revolution
Artificial Neural Networks (Deep Learning) became the dominant approach, leading to breakthroughs in vision and language.

2011: Watson Wins Jeopardy! – IBM’s Watson defeated all-time champions on the quiz show Jeopardy!, demonstrating advanced natural language understanding.

2011: Siri and Virtual Assistants – Apple integrated Siri into the iPhone 4S, making conversational AI a mass-market consumer product.

2012: AlexNet and CNNs – The AlexNet model won the ImageNet competition by a massive margin, proving that deep convolutional neural networks were the future of computer vision.

2014: GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks) – Ian Goodfellow introduced GANs, allowing two neural networks to "compete" to create highly realistic synthetic data, such as deepfakes.

2016: AlphaGo Defeats Lee Sedol – Google DeepMind’s AlphaGo defeated the world champion in the game of Go, a feat previously thought to be decades away due to the game's complexity.

2017: The Transformer Architecture – Google researchers published "Attention is All You Need," introducing the Transformer model—the foundational architecture for all modern Large Language Models (LLMs).

2018: BERT – Google released BERT, a landmark in natural language processing that allowed models to understand the context of words in a sentence simultaneously rather than in a sequence.

# The 2020s: Generative AI and Foundation Models
AI has moved from specialized tasks to "Generative AI," capable of creating human-like text, images, and code.

2020: GPT-3 – OpenAI released GPT-3, which showed that massive scaling of Transformer models could produce remarkably human-like writing and reasoning.

2020: AlphaFold 2 – DeepMind’s AlphaFold solved a 50-year-old challenge in biology by predicting protein structures, a major milestone for AI in scientific discovery.

2022: Image Generators (DALL-E 2 / Midjourney) – The release of high-quality text-to-image models brought creative generative AI to the public mainstream.

2022: ChatGPT – The launch of ChatGPT became the fastest-growing consumer application in history, moving AI from a backend tool to a direct conversational partner for millions.

2023: Multimodal Models – The release of GPT-4 and Gemini marked the shift to multimodality, where a single AI can process and generate text, images, audio, and video simultaneously.

2024: AI Video Generation (Sora) – The introduction of Sora and similar models demonstrated the ability of AI to generate highly realistic, complex video scenes from simple text prompts.

2025: Agentic AI Systems – The current frontier involves AI Agents that don't just chat, but can autonomously navigate websites, use tools, and complete multi-step tasks without constant human intervention.
