When a government decides it knows better than the market how to run the economy, it begins deciding who gets what, who works where, how much bread costs, and how much land a family may keep. This often begins with the stated intention of ending poverty and inequality forever.

For most of the twentieth century, that question was tested at enormous human cost across half the globe. The record of what happened comes down to us today from two very different kinds of people. Economists explained why it would fail before it actually failed. Witnesses who lived through it wrote down what it actually looked like when it did. The threads of theory and testimony turned out to be telling the same story from opposite ends.

**Part One: The Idea That Something Was Wrong**

Long before the Soviet Union existed, a French writer named Frédéric Bastiat put his finger on a problem that would define the next hundred years. In 1850, in a short book called *The Law*, he argued that governments have one legitimate job, which is protecting people's life, liberty, and property. The moment government starts taking property from one group of people by force and handing it to another group, even with the best of intentions and even if a majority votes for it, it has stopped protecting rights and started committing what Bastiat called legal plunder. The theft does not stop being theft merely because a law says it is allowed.

This was a basic warning about what happens when a society decides that the state may use force to reorganize who owns what, long before communism existed as a governing system. Nearly a century later, that warning would be tested on a scale Bastiat could not have imagined.

**Part Two: A Boy Raised to Believe**

Malcolm Muggeridge was born in England in 1903 into a family that embodied socialism in the flesh. His father was a Labour politician, and his household orbited around the movement's founding generation. His aunt by marriage was Beatrice Webb, who together with her husband Sidney had helped invent British socialist theory itself through the Fabian Society. The family's social circle included Ramsay MacDonald, who would become Britain's first Labour prime minister. Decades later, when Muggeridge wrote his memoirs, he titled the chapter about his childhood "A Socialist Upbringing" because he grew up assuming that history was moving toward a better, planned, collectively owned world, viewing this as natural progress the way electricity or vaccines were progress.

Muggeridge did not go to the Soviet Union as a skeptic looking for evidence to confirm his suspicions. He went as a believer hoping to see the dream in action.

**Part Three: The Believer Goes to Look**

After a restless start to adulthood teaching in India and then in Egypt, Muggeridge landed the job that would change his life. He became the Moscow correspondent for the Manchester Guardian, one of Britain's most respected left leaning newspapers, in the early 1930s. He arrived in the Soviet Union at the very moment Stalin's government was forcing millions of independent farmers off their land and into collective farms through a policy known as collectivization.

What Muggeridge found did not match anything he had been raised to expect. In Ukraine, he saw a famine caused directly by the state seizing grain from farmers who were already starving. The state used this seized grain to feed cities and fund industrialization while sealing off the region so word could not easily get out. Historians today estimate the death toll from this famine, now often called the Holodomor, at somewhere between three and five million people in Ukraine alone. Even the most conservative estimates place the toll well over a million. Muggeridge smuggled his dispatches out and reported the empty villages, the starving families, and the deadly policy he had seen.

**Part Four: The Machinery of Denial**

The people who destroyed Muggeridge's credibility were not strangers but the intellectual establishment he had been raised inside of.

Walter Duranty, the New York Times Moscow correspondent, flatly denied the famine was happening and dismissed reports like Muggeridge's as exaggeration. Duranty's denials were influential enough and comforting enough to readers who wanted to believe in the Soviet project that he was awarded a Pulitzer Prize for his Moscow reporting. The Times has never returned this prize, though it remains one of journalism's most notorious embarrassments. Muggeridge later called Duranty the greatest liar he had ever met.

Back home, Muggeridge's own family circle turned on him. George Bernard Shaw, the famous playwright, had just returned from a guided tour of the USSR and publicly described granaries overflowing with grain tended by healthy workers. This scene was stage managed for foreign visitors and swallowed whole. Beatrice Webb, Muggeridge's own aunt by marriage, publicly denied that forced labor even existed in the Soviet Union. Privately, when pressed, she reportedly admitted that people disappeared in Russia. She knew the truth and said otherwise in public because the dream mattered more than the report from the ground.

Muggeridge spent years professionally and socially punished for telling the truth. Decades later, an endorsement from Alexander Solzhenitsyn, who had survived the Soviet camp system himself, alongside vindication from a 1990 biography of Duranty titled Stalin's Apologist, finally credited what Muggeridge had risked his career to report. By then, he was dead.

**Part Five: A Different Vantage Point Vienna**

While Muggeridge was in Moscow discovering the gap between socialist theory and socialist reality, an Austrian economist named Friedrich Hayek was watching a related danger unfold at home. Hayek had already left for the London School of Economics in 1931, and when Nazi Germany annexed Austria in 1938 in an event known as the Anschluss, he did not return. He watched fascism consume his homeland from abroad, and later he watched the war against it fought in the name of central planning.

In 1944, Hayek published *The Road to Serfdom*, building an argument about the concentration of control itself rather than "left versus right". His central claim stated that no government planner, no matter how intelligent or well meaning, can possibly gather the scattered knowledge of millions of individual people, including their preferences, local conditions, and trade offs. A decentralized price system handles this information automatically. Attempting to centrally plan an economy requires increasing coercion to force reality to match the plan. That coercion, once concentrated in the state's hands, tends to slide toward tyranny regardless of which ideology started it. Hayek had watched fascism rise in the name of national unity and control, and he warned that socialist central planning carried the same structural risk dressed in gentler language.

This theoretical half of the story is an argument built from reasoning about knowledge and power, published from the safety of a library by a man who had personally watched what happens when a country hands total control to people who believe they know best.

**Part Six: A Marriage Inside the Machine**

If Muggeridge was an outside observer and Hayek was a theorist watching from exile, Margarete Buber Neumann's story places her inside the machine.

Buber Neumann was a committed German communist married to Heinz Neumann, a senior figure in the German Communist Party. When Hitler took power in 1933, the couple fled to Moscow as political refugees, seeing it as the promised land actively fighting fascism. Loyalty to the Party did not protect Heinz Neumann from the Party itself. In 1937, during Stalin's Great Purge, Neumann was arrested by the Soviet secret police and executed before the year was out. He was a devoted communist killed by the revolution he had served.

The following year, Margarete herself was arrested. She was held first in Lubyanka, the notorious Moscow prison, and then sent to Karaganda, a vast forced labor camp in Soviet Central Asia. She was imprisoned for being married to the wrong person at the wrong political moment, becoming a direct victim of the Soviet system.

**Part Seven: The Handoff**

In August 1939, Stalin's Soviet Union signed a non aggression pact with Nazi Germany. Two ideologies that presented themselves to the world as mortal enemies quietly agreed to carve up Eastern Europe between them and leave each other alone for the time being.

As a side effect of this new alliance, the Soviet government handed a group of German prisoners directly over to the Gestapo in 1940. Margarete Buber Neumann was among them. A woman imprisoned by Stalin's regime for years was transferred straight into Hitler's regime without ever being free for a single day. She went from a Soviet labor camp to Ravensbrück, the Nazi concentration camp for women, where she would spend the next five years.

This sequence reveals a communist true believer, imprisoned by communists, handed by communists directly to fascists without a moment of freedom in between. Jailers on either side of that handoff would have vigorously denied being the same kind of regime as the other, yet her body experienced their fundamental similarities.

Buber Neumann survived Ravensbrück and wrote *Under Two Dictators* after the war, providing an eyewitness account of the machinery of both the Gulag and the Nazi camp system. It came from someone with the terrible authority of having endured both. Historians respect the work as a standard text of the period, yet it remains virtually unknown among ordinary readers in the English speaking world. It is a strange injustice that a document historians rely extensively upon remains unread by the public.

**Part Eight: The True Believer's Confession**

Arthur Koestler's path ran parallel to Buber Neumann's experience. Born in Budapest in 1905, Koestler joined the German Communist Party in 1931, believing he was joining the side of history's progress. He worked for the cause through the 1930s, including dangerous underground activity in Spain during its civil war, where he was imprisoned by Franco's forces under sentence of death.

By 1938, Koestler had broken with the Communist Party, disillusioned largely by what the purges revealed about the gap between communist ideals and practice. In 1949, he joined five other prominent writers, all former communists from different countries, in a collection called *The God That Failed*. Each contributed an essay explaining what had gone wrong with communism and why intelligent, morally serious people had believed in it in the first place. Understanding why smart people fall for these systems turned out to be at least as important as documenting the failure itself.

Three years later, in 1952, Koestler published *Arrow in the Blue*, tracing his life from childhood through his 1931 decision to join the Party. This deep autobiography explored the psychological road that leads a person toward intense ideological belief, and eventually away from it.

**Part Nine: Fiction Carries What the Reports Could Not**

Journalism and memoir document facts, while fiction can make readers feel what those facts actually mean. That job fell to George Orwell, who had seen communist tactics firsthand while fighting in the Spanish Civil War. He emerged deeply suspicious of how revolutionary movements treated truth itself.

*Animal Farm* retold the story of the Russian Revolution as a fable where animals overthrow their human owner in the name of equality, only to watch a new ruling class of pigs recreate the same tyranny under new slogans. His final novel, *1984*, imagined a state that controls behavior and rewrites history so thoroughly that objective, checkable truth becomes impossible to hold onto. The idea that whoever controls the past controls the future takes the smuggled famine reports and camp memories and turns them into something readers feel in their stomachs.

**Part Ten: The Argument Comes Home**

The argument Bastiat and Hayek had built did not stay confined to Europe or to academic journals. In postwar America, Milton Friedman carried the case into the mainstream through his book *Capitalism and Freedom*, and then through *Free to Choose*, a television series that put Hayek's abstract knowledge problem into plain language for a mass audience. Thomas Sowell did similar work in books like *Basic Economics*, walking readers step by step through how price controls, wage floors, and well intentioned interventions tend to produce the precise opposite of what their designers promised. This pattern of good intentions colliding with reality matched what Hayek described in theory and what Muggeridge and Buber Neumann had lived in fact.

Decades after *The Road to Serfdom* had been dismissed by many mainstream intellectuals as alarmist, the argument received formal vindication. In November 1983, President Ronald Reagan invited the elderly Hayek to the White House to record his admiration. In 1991, President George H.W. Bush awarded Hayek the Presidential Medal of Freedom, citing him as a thinker who had explored the promise of liberty more thoroughly than anyone of his age. Hayek was too frail to receive it in person, but the argument that had once been a minority position within economics had become something close to consensus at the highest levels of American government by the end of the Cold War.

**Part Eleven: Weaving the Thread**

An idea stated plainly by Bastiat in 1850 argued that forcing people to give up what is theirs by legal means remains plunder. Hayek developed this into a fuller theory showing that central planning cannot gather the required knowledge, leading to coercion that slides toward tyranny.

Muggeridge, raised inside the socialist movement, went to Moscow expecting to see the dream and instead saw a deadly famine. An establishment that chose comfort over honesty surrounded him for years. Duranty won a Pulitzer for his denial, while Webb and Shaw publicly repeated stories they privately suspected were false. Buber Neumann's book ended up respected by historians but remained virtually unread by everyone else.

Orwell's fiction gave millions of ordinary readers a felt sense of what the witnesses had lived through, while Friedman and Sowell popularized Hayek's careful economic arguments. The White House meeting in 1983 and the Medal of Freedom in 1991 marked the moment the theoretical half of the story finally received formal recognition, while the eyewitness half largely still has not.

**Part Twelve: The Argument Returns**

For decades after Reagan and Bush honored Hayek, socialism functioned mainly as an accusation in American politics, a word one candidate threw at another rather than a label anyone claimed for themselves. That changed in 2016, when Senator Bernie Sanders of Vermont ran for the Democratic presidential nomination as a self described democratic socialist and did not lose by enough to be dismissed as fringe. His campaign, combined with Donald Trump's election that same year, drove a wave of new interest in the Democratic Socialists of America, the organization born from Michael Harrington's 1982 merger. DSA's membership, which had sat around six thousand people for years, began to climb. By 2026 it had passed one hundred and twenty thousand dues paying members across roughly two hundred chapters in forty eight states, making it the largest socialist organization in American history.

This new generation draws a careful line around itself, and it is the same line that separates every figure in the earlier parts of this story from one another. Unlike the revolutionary communism that killed Heinz Neumann and starved Ukraine, democratic socialism as DSA defines it pursues its goals entirely through elections, legislation, and organizing, not by seizing state power through force. Whether that distinction matters as much as its members believe is a question this essay cannot yet answer, because the story is still being written.

**Part Thirteen: A New Generation, A New Test**

The clearest proof of concept arrived in November 2025, when Zohran Mamdani, a democratic socialist and political newcomer, was elected mayor of New York City, defeating former governor Andrew Cuomo. The win gave the movement something it had never had before, a chief executive of the country's largest city.

What followed in 2026 was not a single breakthrough but a string of them. Melat Kiros, a first time challenger endorsed by DSA, defeated fifteen term incumbent Diana DeGette in Colorado's Democratic primary. Chris Rabb, running unopposed in the general election in Pennsylvania, positioned himself to become only the second DSA member of Congress alongside Rashida Tlaib of Michigan. Janeese Lewis George won the Democratic mayoral primary in Washington DC, all but assuring her election in a heavily Democratic city. In Los Angeles, city council member Nithya Raman advanced to a mayoral runoff, and in Seattle, Katie Wilson was already governing as mayor. Beneath these national names, DSA built a growing bench in state legislatures in New York, Minnesota, Colorado, Delaware, and Wisconsin, along with more than two hundred and fifty members holding elected office across forty states.

**Part Fourteen: What This Generation Is Asking For**

In 2026, DSA released an updated platform, and its ambitions went well beyond the familiar progressive policy list of Medicare for All, a thirty two hour workweek with no reduction in pay, cancellation of student debt, and universal rent control. The platform also called for eliminating the United States Senate outright and replacing the presidency and the Supreme Court with an executive and judiciary chosen by, and subordinate to, Congress.

This is worth setting beside Part One of this story. Bastiat's warning concerned the redistribution of property. Hayek's warning concerned the concentration of economic decision making. The 2026 platform reaches further than either, proposing to restructure the constitutional architecture that divides power between branches of government in the first place, not by force but through the same democratic and legislative channels the movement insists on using for everything else. Whether pursuing that goal through ballots and amendments changes the nature of the risk Hayek described, or simply changes the method by which power might eventually concentrate, is exactly the kind of question the earlier parts of this story were built to raise.

**Part Fifteen: The Difference This Time, and the Unanswered Question**

The case for taking the distinction seriously is straightforward. Nothing in DSA's rise resembles the mechanics of Part Six or Part Seven of this story. There is no secret police, no purge, no handoff between dictatorships. Candidates win primaries, lose primaries, and answer to voters who can remove them. That is a structural difference from the systems Muggeridge, Buber Neumann, and Koestler lived inside, and it is not a small one.

The case for caution is also straightforward, and it comes from several directions at once. Hayek's argument was never really about how power is acquired. It was about what concentrated control over an economy requires once it exists, regardless of the ballot box that produced it. Centrist critics within the Democratic Party, including strategists at the think tank Third Way, argue that DSA's wins are concentrated almost entirely in safe, deep blue districts, and question whether the same coalition can win anywhere Republicans are competitive. Public polling complicates the movement's own account of itself. A Pew Research survey in June 2026 found DSA viewed unfavorably by wider margins than favorably even among the generation it claims to represent, twenty eight percent favorable against forty percent unfavorable among adults eighteen to twenty nine, and worse among adults thirty to forty four. Inside the organization, rapid growth has produced its own strain, an online culture of ideological dispute sharp enough that even Alexandria Ocasio Cortez, arguably DSA's most successful national figure, has faced accusations from within the movement of selling out for working inside the mainstream party. Separately, and on a different axis of controversy entirely, DSA's 2025 adoption of anti Zionism as a core membership principle has drawn criticism from organizations including the Anti Defamation League, which argues that some rhetoric from DSA affiliated figures has crossed from criticism of Israeli policy into antisemitism, a charge DSA and its defenders reject. Further to the right, some commentators have drawn an explicit and contested comparison between DSA's strategy of building strength inside an existing party's primaries and infrastructure and the tactics Bolsheviks used inside the broader Russian socialist movement before 1917, a parallel DSA's supporters would firmly reject as describing a democratic electoral coalition as though it were a conspiracy.

None of these critiques proves the movement will repeat the twentieth century's worst outcomes. None of them can be dismissed either. That is the honest state of the argument in the middle of 2026.

**Part Sixteen: An Unfinished Chapter**

Nikolaus Wachsmann had the advantage of decades of hindsight when he wrote his introduction to *Under Two Dictators*, distance enough to know how the story of Buber Neumann's century actually ended. Nobody writing about 2026 has that same distance yet.

The questions running underneath every earlier part of this story are being asked again. Can concentrated power be trusted with more control over an economy than any individual planner or market can responsibly hold. Will the institutions positioned to report the results tell the truth about what happens as it happens, rather than protect a comforting story, the way Duranty and Shaw and, for a while, Webb did not. Will testimony, when it comes, reach people in time to matter. A generation with no living memory of how the twentieth century answered those questions is now answering them for itself, ballot by ballot, this time with the process point in its favor and the outcome still unknown.

That is where this story currently stops, mid sentence, waiting on an ending nobody alive today is yet positioned to write.
