California recently celebrated a substantial funding allocation of $900 million as part of a larger effort to address homelessness. Over recent years, the state has committed an impressive total of $24 billion to this cause. However, a recent audit has highlighted a critical issue: agencies have not tracked the outcomes of this significant expenditure. Instead of focusing on actual improvements in people's lives, the state continues to rely on administrative metrics. These generic measures include tracking demographics, funding scattered-site permanent housing, and subsidizing emergency shelters. This one-size-fits-all approach overlooks the complex realities faced by those experiencing homelessness.

The truth is that the unsheltered population comprises individuals with varying clinical and social conditions. Each person has a unique set of needs that generic solutions cannot address. For instance, someone whose nervous system is struggling cannot effectively manage the responsibilities of independent living. Providing a stable home without first addressing their health and social conditions is likely to lead to failure. This example illustrates the necessity of targeted intervention before placing individuals in housing.

To truly address this crisis, we need a clinical engineering framework, one that focuses on the specific needs of individuals. This approach requires a Phase Zero metabolic stabilization, which means ensuring that a person's basic health needs are met before they are offered housing. Additionally, creating specialized environments designed to support these individuals is essential. Accountability metrics must be established to measure success. A housing retention rate of at least 85 percent over 24 months and a proven decrease in emergency costs for each resident are essential benchmarks.

Instead of celebrating the large sums allocated to this issue, we should focus on what those funds accomplish. Success must not be defined solely by the amount of money spent. It should be measured by tangible outcomes that lead to long-term improvements in the lives of those affected. Without a shift from broad administrative intent to focused, clinical responses, the billions invested in this crisis will only perpetuate a cycle of failure.
